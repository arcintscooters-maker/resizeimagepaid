How to Photograph Small Items Like Jewellery and Electronics

Photographing small products presents a unique set of challenges. Jewellery, electronics components, watches, miniature crafts, and other compact items demand precision that larger products do not. Details that are invisible to the casual eye become prominent at close range -- dust, fingerprints, uneven lighting, and shallow depth of field can all undermine an otherwise good image.

The stakes are high. Customers buying a piece of jewellery or a small electronic device need to see fine detail before committing to a purchase. They want to examine the texture of a metal finish, the clarity of a gemstone, the layout of ports and buttons, or the quality of a clasp. If your images cannot deliver that level of detail, they will buy from a competitor whose images can.

This guide covers the equipment, techniques, and workflows that will help you capture small items with the clarity and precision they require.

Choosing the Right Lens

For dedicated small-item photography, a macro lens is the most important investment you can make. A true macro lens offers a 1:1 magnification ratio, meaning it can reproduce a subject at its actual size on the camera sensor. This lets you fill the frame with a ring, a circuit board, or a watch face and capture every detail sharply.

The most popular focal lengths for product macro work are 90mm to 105mm. These provide a comfortable working distance between the lens and the subject, which makes lighting easier and avoids casting shadows from the lens itself. Models such as the Canon EF 100mm f/2.8L and the Nikon 105mm f/2.8 are widely used by professionals, but more affordable options deliver comparable results at a fraction of the cost.

Budget Alternatives

  • Extension tubes. These mount between your camera body and an existing lens, forcing it to focus at closer distances. They can be purchased for as little as twenty pounds and work with lenses you already own. The trade-off is reduced light reaching the sensor and a narrower focus range.
  • Close-up filters. These screw onto the front of a standard lens like a magnifying glass. Quality varies significantly -- invest in multi-element diopters for the sharpest results.
  • Smartphone macro attachments. Clip-on macro lenses for phones are inexpensive and surprisingly capable for basic close-up work, though they require you to shoot extremely close to the subject.

Camera Settings for Small Items

Macro photography requires more careful control of camera settings than standard product photography. The margins are smaller and mistakes are amplified.

Aperture and Depth of Field

Depth of field -- the range of distance that appears sharp in an image -- becomes extremely shallow at macro distances. At 1:1 magnification, even f/8 may only keep a few millimetres of the subject in focus. This is the defining challenge of small-item photography.

  • For most small product shots, use an aperture between f/8 and f/11. This provides a reasonable depth of field while maintaining good sharpness.
  • For larger small items (a watch face, a small electronic device), f/11 to f/16 will keep more of the subject sharp.
  • Avoid going beyond f/22. At very small apertures, diffraction reduces overall image sharpness, defeating the purpose of stopping down.

ISO and Shutter Speed

Keep ISO as low as possible -- ideally between 100 and 400 -- to minimise noise and maintain clean detail. Because you will be using smaller apertures that let in less light, shutter speeds will be slower. This makes a tripod essential rather than optional.

Use a cable release, wireless remote, or the camera's self-timer to trigger the shutter. At macro distances, even the vibration from pressing the shutter button can cause blur.

Lighting Small Items

Lighting is critical for all product photography, but small items -- particularly those with reflective or transparent surfaces -- demand extra care.

The Challenge of Reflective Surfaces

Jewellery, watches, and polished electronics reflect everything around them. Without careful lighting, your images will show the reflection of your camera, your lights, and your studio ceiling instead of the product's surface finish.

The solution is diffusion. Rather than pointing a light directly at the product, pass it through a translucent material first. This creates a large, soft light source that wraps around the item and produces smooth, controlled reflections rather than harsh spots.

Lighting Setups

  • Light tent or light box. This is the single most useful piece of equipment for small-item photography. A light tent surrounds the product with translucent white fabric, creating perfectly even, diffused illumination from all sides. Prices range from twenty to two hundred pounds, or you can build one from a cardboard box and baking paper.
  • Two-softbox setup. Position two softboxes at forty-five degree angles either side of the product. This creates gentle, directional light with soft shadows that reveal form and texture.
  • Natural light. A north-facing window provides consistent, diffused daylight throughout the day. Place the item near the window and use a white reflector on the opposite side to fill shadows. This works well for jewellery with a warmer, more natural feel.

Lighting Gems and Stones

Gemstones require particular attention. Their value is communicated through sparkle, clarity, and colour -- all of which are controlled by lighting. A small, focused light source above and slightly behind the stone will create the internal reflections that make gems come alive. Experiment with the angle until you see the brilliance you want, then lock the light in position.

Focus Stacking for Maximum Sharpness

When depth of field is not sufficient to render the entire product sharp in a single exposure, focus stacking is the professional solution. The technique involves capturing multiple images of the same subject, each focused at a slightly different distance, and then combining them in software to produce a single image that is sharp throughout.

  1. Mount your camera on a sturdy tripod and compose the shot.
  2. Set focus to the nearest point of the subject.
  3. Take a photograph, then adjust the focus slightly deeper into the subject.
  4. Repeat until you have covered the entire depth of the item. This might require anywhere from ten to fifty frames depending on the subject's depth and your magnification.
  5. Combine the images using software such as Adobe Photoshop (auto-blend layers), Helicon Focus, or Zerene Stacker.

Focus stacking is time-consuming but produces results that no single exposure can match. For high-value items like fine jewellery, the investment in time is justified by the quality of the final image.

Preparation and Handling

At macro distances, every imperfection is magnified. A speck of dust that is invisible to the naked eye will look like a boulder on a gemstone. A fingerprint on polished metal will dominate the image.

  • Wear microfibre gloves when handling jewellery and polished items to avoid leaving fingerprints.
  • Clean every surface with a microfibre cloth before shooting. Use compressed air to remove particles from crevices and settings.
  • Use Blu Tack or museum putty to prop up rings, earrings, and other items that will not stand on their own. Position it behind or beneath the item where it will not be visible in the final image.
  • Check at 100% zoom on your camera's screen or tethered computer before moving to the next product. Problems that are invisible in the viewfinder become obvious at full resolution.

Backgrounds for Small Products

The background sets the tone for the image and should never compete with the product.

  • White is the safest and most versatile choice. It is clean, professional, highlights fine details, and is required by most e-commerce platforms for primary listing images.
  • Black creates drama and contrast, which works well for luxury items like watches and fine jewellery. It makes metallic finishes and gemstones pop.
  • Textured surfaces -- slate, marble, wood -- add visual interest for lifestyle shots but must be chosen carefully to complement rather than overpower the product.
  • Gradient backgrounds (light fading to slightly darker) add depth without distraction and are popular for electronics and tech accessories.

Photographing Electronics

Small electronics present their own challenges beyond those of jewellery. Screens, indicator lights, ports, and buttons all need to be clearly visible and accurately represented.

  • Show screens in both on and off states. A powered-on screen demonstrates the interface, while a powered-off shot shows the physical design without glare.
  • Highlight ports and connections. Customers need to see what cables and accessories will fit. Photograph these at close range with clear focus.
  • Manage LED reflections. Matte surfaces are easier to light than glossy ones. For glossy electronics, use a light tent or position lights at angles that minimise direct reflections on the screen.
  • Include scale references. Small electronics can be difficult to judge for size. Include a shot with the item held in a hand or placed next to a common object like a coin or pen.

Smartphone Tips for Small Items

If you are shooting with a smartphone, modern devices offer surprisingly good macro capabilities. Many flagship phones now include dedicated macro modes that switch to an ultra-wide lens at close focus distances.

  • Use the phone's built-in macro mode or portrait mode for close-up shots.
  • Tap the screen to lock focus on the product, then adjust exposure if needed.
  • A phone tripod is essential -- hand-holding at macro distances produces blurred results.
  • Clean the lens before every session.
  • Consider a clip-on macro lens for even greater magnification, though be aware that very cheap attachments can reduce image quality at the edges.

Post-Processing and Final Preparation

After shooting, process your images to correct any remaining issues. Adjust white balance to ensure metals and gemstones appear their true colour. Remove any dust spots or minor blemishes using the healing or clone tool. Sharpen selectively to enhance fine details without creating artefacts.

Building a Repeatable Workflow

The key to efficient small-item photography is building a repeatable process. Document your setup: the position of your lights, the camera settings, the background, and the angles you need. Create a shot list for each product type so you can work through items systematically rather than improvising each time.

With a consistent workflow, you can photograph multiple items in a single session, maintain visual consistency across your catalogue, and dramatically reduce the time between receiving new stock and listing it for sale. The initial investment in learning these techniques pays for itself many times over in the quality and efficiency of your product photography.
